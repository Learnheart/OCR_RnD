A-2: Draw a great circle through two of the points. There are two closed hemispheres with the great circle as boundary, and each of the other three points lies in one of them. By the pigeonhole principle, two of these three points lie in the same hemisphere, and that hemisphere thus contains four of the five given points.

Note: by a similar argument, one can prove that among any $n+1$ points on an $n$-dimensional sphere, some $n+1$ of them lie on a closed hemisphere. (One cannot get by with only $n+2$ points; put three at the vertices of a regular simplex.) Namely, any $n$ of the points lie in a great circle, which divides the boundary of two hemispheres; one of the remaining three points, some two lie in the same hemisphere.
