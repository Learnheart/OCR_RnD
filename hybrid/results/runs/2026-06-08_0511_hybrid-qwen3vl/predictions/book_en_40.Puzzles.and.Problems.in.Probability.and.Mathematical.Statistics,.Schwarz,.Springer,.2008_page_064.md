3.11 The Optimal Level of Supply

a. Clearly, if he orders no milk at all, there will be no gain and no loss, G(0) = 0. For very small c, the man will almost certainly sell his entire lot c, thereby gaining c(a - b). Thus, for small c, the net gain G will increase linearly with slope a - b and thus be positive. For very large c, almost surely not all stocked milk will be sold. In this situation, each additional unit stocked will impose an additional loss of b. Thus, for c large, G will decrease linearly with slope -b. Therefore, G must have at least one positive maximum for c > 0.

b. If he orders c units and sells only some of them, 0 ≤ d ≤ c, then his conditional expected net gain G(c) will be equal to d(a - b) - (c - d)b because his gain from the d units sold is d(a - b), and the cost of the remaining c - d unused units is (c - d)b. The other hand, if d ≥ c he will sell all c units, yielding G(c) = c(a - b).
