(i) If $A$ and $B$ are at the same side of $AB$, $ACD$ was to be acute, then $AC$ and $BD$ would have to be at least 1.0 unit apart. But this would imply that the distance from $AC$ to $AD$ is at least 1.0 unit, which contradicts the given condition that the distance from $AC$ to $AD$ is less than 1.0 unit.

(ii) If $A$ and $B$ are on opposite sides of $AB$, then $ACD$ is acute and, accordingly, the same is true of $BCD$. Since $ACD$ is a convex quadrilateral, at most one of the triangles $ACD$ or $BCD$ contains the center $O$, and hence at most one of the triangles $ACD$ or $BCD$ contains the center $O$. On the other hand, by the choice of $A$ and $B$ and the set of triangles, we can choose any triangle that contains the center $O$. Thus, there are at most $n(n-1)$ such triangles.

(iii) Those having neither $A$ nor $B$ as one of their vertices: by induction hypothesis, there are at most $\frac{1}{4}(n-1)(n-2)$ such triangles.

Adding the contributions of the three cases above, we conclude that the number of acute triangles having its vertices in three of the 24 + 1 given points is at most

$k + i(k - 1) + \frac{1}{4}(n-1)(n-2) \leq k + i(k - 1) + \frac{1}{4}(n-1)(n-2) + 1.$

Problems: Sect.4.2

(1) (India) Prove that, for every $n \geq 6$, every square can be partitioned into $n$ other squares.

(2) (Brazil) Given a natural number $n > 1$, we write a real number of modulus less than 1 in each cell of an $n \times n$ chessboard, in such a way that the sum of the numbers written in the four cells of any $2 \times 2$ square is equal to 0. If $n$ is odd, show that the sum of the numbers written in the $n$ cells less than 1.

(3) (Togo) Point $O$ is situated in the interior of the convex polygon $A_1A_2 \cdots A_n$. Consider all of the angles $\angle A_iOA_j$, with distinct $i \leq j \leq n$. Prove that at least $n - 1$ of them are not acute.

(4) (TT) In a convex polygon $P$ some diagonals were drawn, such that no two of them intersect in the interior of $P$. Show that there are at least two vertices of $P$ such that none of the traced diagonals is incident to one of these two vertices.

(5) (Hungary) In an $n \times n$ chessboard we have a certain number of towers (at most one tower for each 1 $\times$ 1 square). A tower can move from one square to another if and only if these two squares belong to a single line or column of the board and there are no other towers in between. In other words, we say that any two of these towers can attack the other. Prove that we can paint the towers with one of three colors in such a way that no tower can attack another tower of the same color.
